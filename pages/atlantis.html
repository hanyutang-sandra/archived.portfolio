What is it?

Atlantis Hub Information Board

A data-driven environmental display board that allows users to:

Learn about the travelling as well as transferring information quickly and clearly.
Get first-hand information about emergent broadcasting.
UGet to know the new program promoted by the Transportation Municipality.

Background Information

The 'Atlantis Hub' is an imaginary transportation hub on the center of San Juan Island, which is a real island of Washington State.
There are multiple islands, large and small surrounding the San Juan Island, like Orcas Island, Shaw Island and Lopez Island. Because of the complex geographical condition, in order to travel among multiple islands as well as the mainland, residents and travellers need to effectively utilize multiple transportation methods.
How to integrate and display this complex information effectively and clearly is a large painpoint of the user and this is the major problem we are trying to solve.

Research

Raw Data Understanding

Atlantis Hub is a transferring section at the middle of the main island. It gathers the travelling information for flights, trains and ferry. The raw dataset was presented to us in the form of an Excel file, with three sub-sheets for flight, train and ferry information.

Despite that the data in the form is clearly and correctly listed, in reality, travellers are easily overwhelmed by the data they do not need when looking into such form. A redesign of the display of the data is needed.

Static Design

Paper Sketching

We began the design process by making thumbnail sketches. The sketches were focusing on the selection, grouping and display of the data.

Draft 1

For draft 1, we attempted to do a divide and conquer technique, asking each person to deeply explore one section of the map. We had trouble combining the individual parts into a cohesive whole and learned very quickly that we needed to explore the data more deeply together.

Draft 2

We initially began by abstracting the different items, exploring how we could highlight the connections visually. We moved to a more abstract creation of the map and explored more condensed tables.

We compiled a more condensed table and abstract map, choosing to use the top half of the screen to display all trip information and the bottom half of the screen to highlight each island. Through this highlighting, we wanted to explicitly showcase the PFT Pass and connections from one type of transportation to the other. Once we viewed this on the screen in class, we realized that certain elements needed to be rearranged and de-emphasized in order to promote the correct relationships for the viewer. For instance, the trip presented at the bottom of the screen and intended to inform viewers ways to get to Stuart Island, but instead appear to be current trips.

Draft 3

In draft 3, we introduced color, attempting to use it as a way to differentiate the types of travel information (green for planes, blue for ferries, and red for trains). We struggled most with how to create a map that was clear enough to communicate the different transportation opportunities while also communicating the geographic relationships. We also began experimenting with different ways to represent the PFT Pass

Draft 4

We refined our design and focused on a new type of flagging method to denote PFT Pass eligible rides. We also finalized our map design. Additionally, we began adding in animations. We received feedback that the animations should be a little more commuicative to the viewer, letting them know when screens might be about to change. Additionally, the bottom half or the screen had some unclear relationships to the top half. In our next and final draft, we attempted to rectify just that.

Static Screen Final Design

For our final design, we really focused on clarifying the distinctions between the top and bottom halfs of the overall screen. The top half is meant to communicate all rides, with flights and ferry rides organized by destination. The train information is abstracted, highlighting the overall patterns of the rides. The bottom of the screen s lighter, communicating that it is independent from the top. This section of the screen highlights each island and the potential transportation paths you can take to get there.

Here is the information board you will see in the Atlantis Hub

Our final animations focused on being noticeable yet not straining on the user. The map fades in and out to the next island, but the title and times swipe in to draw attention. A cancelled flight is flipped over, and a new ticker comes in to show an update. The times, fade and out, but stop briefly on a blank screen to show that they are changing.
